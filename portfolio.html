LINC Portal

AUDIENCE
  • Newly relocated hires in need of assistance relocating and acclimating to the Lehigh Valley, referred by partner companies
  • HR representatives and recruiters from partner companies such as Air Products, B. Braun, Crayola, and Lutron Electronics.
BACKGROUND

The LINC Portal has two sides: an "employer side" for partner companies, and a "client side" for newly relocated hires.

On the employer side, hiring representatives from partner companies can refer new hires for LINC services, using the online referral form. All referral form submissions are stored in the WordPress database, allowing for easy, ongoing reporting on metrics such as cost and services utilized.

On the client side, new hires referred by partner companies can navigate the resources and information necessary for a smooth transition to their new home.

LINC Resource Web App & Database

BACKGROUND

I first conceived this web app upon seeing a critical issue that had been hindering LINC's efficiency since its founding: providing community resources to clients is at the heart of LINC services, but without a database or consistent formatting tool, LINC client coordinators relied on storing information in various spreadsheets and presenting deliverables to clients in poorly formatted text documents. This lack of necessary tools resulted in an exorbitant amount of hours spent searching for and often recreating existing content. Whereas previously it might take an average of 4 hours to research and format resources for a single client, this app made it possible to do so in 1 hour, using the filtering and lookup functions of saved resources.

The design of the underlying database required acute examination and thoughtful design. Being one of two employees who have been with LINC since its founding, I was intimately familiar with the complexities of the information and processes we work with as well as how this continues to change, so I tried to convert this experience into intuitive and efficient functions.

As LINC client coordinators have a great deal of tacit knowledge regarding their work and are accustomed to carrying out their operations without the use of such technological tools, an iterative design process was necessary to ensure an intuitive and efficient user experience. We have ongoing conversations to discuss how the app is working, how it isn't, and how it could be improved.

Topicalizer Data Visualization

AUDIENCE
  • Academics and researchers
  • Autistic individuals
  • Parents and caretakers of autistic children
BACKGROUND

The "Topicalizer" is a data visualization tool designed to model recurring topics within large bodies of text-based content, as well as each topic's prevalence and coherence. Currently this tool is being used to represent over 100,000 documents: journal entries by Bohemian poet Thom Gunn, and blog entries by parents of autistic children.

The goal of this visualization is to highlight patterns within each data collection. Users can see at a glance the breadth of topics discussed, how common each topic is, as well as the relationship between various topics. There is an option to toggle between arbitrary color representation as well as a view in which color value scales according to the coherence value of each topic. Upon taking a closer look, users can view the relevance of individual entries within each topic, as well as read each individual entry. With regard to the autism blogs data collection specifically, care was taken to ensure that the user experience would be sensory-friendly (e.g. easy to read fonts, mild color saturation). UX testing and interviews were conducted with researchers who would be utilizing the tool to ensure that it would be as helpful as possible.

Tarot Keeper

AUDIENCE
  • Tarot card enthusiasts
BACKGROUND

I decided to make this web application because I enjoy reading tarot cards both for myself and for family, and noticed some cards appearing much more frequently than others, so I thought it would be interesting to track and visualize such data over time, and that other tarot card fans might feel similarly.

Since tarot cards can be a fun self-reflection tool, users can not only record which cards they pulled for a tarot reading, but also their personal notes or comments. Users can also view the relative frequency of cards they have pulled over periods of time to see if they notice any trends and form their own interpretations. Eventually I hope to implement text analysis to highlight most common keywords/meanings that come up during each user's readings.

Image of the "Record a new reading" page of the Tarot Keeper web application, where users can select the tarot spread they are recording, name the reading, select the cards pulled for that reading, read the meanings of each card, as well as enter their own notes in a TinyMCE Wysiwig editor.
